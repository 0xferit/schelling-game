The Origin

Meet Thomas Schelling

In 1960, economist Thomas Schelling published The Strategy of Conflict, a landmark study in how people coordinate without explicit communication. He found that, when pushed to guess what others will choose, people drift toward answers that feel prominent or natural. He called these focal points.

Schelling Games turns that idea into a live multiplayer format. Every round asks the same question in a harsher environment: can you find the answer that a room of strangers will independently recognize as the coordination target?

How It Works

The protocol loop

Each game follows the same sealed cycle. Choices are committed before the table can read them, then opened together, then settled by plurality.

01

Commit

Read the prompt, pick your answer, hash it with a salt, and submit. The table can speculate, but it cannot see your actual choice yet.

Blind commitment
02

Reveal

When the commit window closes, everyone reveals the answer behind their hash. Select prompts verify exact options and open-text prompts verify canonical buckets.

Shared reveal window
03

Settle

The largest matching group wins. Ties split the pot. The mechanism rewards whatever answer a room of strangers can independently recognize as obvious.

Plurality payout
Why It Holds

Salience becomes strategy

The mechanism works because every part of the loop points toward the same equilibrium. Plurality rewards convergence, commit-reveal removes reactive copying, and the prompt forces each player to model what the group will find most legible.

Plurality wins

The best move is to identify the answer that feels most shared, not the answer that feels smartest.

Blind commitment

Everyone crosses the same cryptographic boundary before they can inspect the table.

Truth as equilibrium

When the salient answer is also the profitable answer, shared intuition becomes a stable coordination target.

Ethereum Context 2014 / SchellingCoin Mechanism beyond the game

From theory to on-chain mechanism

Vitalik Buterin's early SchellingCoin idea pushed the same incentive shape into blockchain design: reward the report that matches the majority, and honest reporting can become the best strategy available. That logic later shows up in oracle systems, prediction market resolution, dispute systems, and governance coordination.

01

Oracles

Decentralized price feeds rely on independent reporters converging on the same truth.

02

Prediction Markets

Disputed outcomes can resolve through shared reporting instead of a single trusted judge.

03

Decentralized Courts

Jurors are rewarded for arriving at the same verdict without direct coordination.

04

Governance

Protocol communities can coordinate around parameters that feel publicly legible and stable.

The Theory

Why some answers feel inevitable

Why do two strangers drift toward Grand Central? Why does the number 100 pull a room of players toward itself? Some answers feel naturally prominent before anyone has spoken. Schelling called these focal points: answers that become coordination anchors because each person expects the others to notice the same thing.

Salience over originality Mutual expectation over direct planning The crowd converges on what feels obvious
Observed by Schelling
People can often concert their intentions or expectations with others if each knows that the other is trying to do the same.
